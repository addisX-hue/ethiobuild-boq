The Part Most People Miss

Your Permitted Floor Count Depends on Your Street

Most first-time builders assume the number of floors they can build is mainly a design or budget decision. In practice, it's also a site-specific constraint tied to the width of the street the plot faces.

Why this matters before you design

Two plots with identical land area, in the same neighborhood, can have different maximum permitted floor counts simply because one faces a wider street than the other. This isn't something a generic construction budget or design brief will tell you — it's determined by the specific site conditions of the plot itself.

The practical implication: confirm the floor limit tied to your specific plot and street width before your architect finalizes a design or your engineer prices a structural system. Designing first and discovering the street-width restriction afterward means redoing work that could have been avoided.

The Process

Step-by-Step: Getting Your Permit Approved

  1. Prepare your documents

    Gather ownership documents, architectural and structural drawings, soil investigation report, and your BOQ before starting the application — see the full list below.

  2. Submit your application

    Submit the complete document set to the Building Permit Office of your city administration (Addis Ababa City Administration, Hawassa City, or your local equivalent).

  3. Review and approval

    The technical committee reviews your drawings for compliance with EBCS, zoning, and the site-specific restrictions covered above — including the street-width-based floor limit. Budget roughly 15 days for this stage if your documents are complete.

  4. Pay fees and receive your permit

    Once approved, pay the applicable permit fees and collect your approved permit.

  5. Start construction

    Keep the permit visible on site. Periodic inspections during construction should be expected as part of the process.

What To Expect

Realistic Approval Timeline

~15
Days for review and approval, once your documents are complete and submitted

This is specifically the review-and-approval stage — Step 3 above. Missing or incorrect documents are what typically push this timeline out further, since an incomplete application has to be corrected and resubmitted rather than reviewed straight through.

Preparation

Documents You'll Need

  • Ownership certificate or land title (or lease agreement, where applicable)
  • Architectural drawings — floor plans, elevations, sections
  • Structural drawings and calculations, stamped by a licensed engineer
  • Soil investigation report, particularly for multi-storey buildings
  • Bill of Quantities (BOQ) — strengthens your application and supports accurate cost declaration
  • Completed application form from your city administration's building permit office

Since the floor count itself depends on your specific plot's street width, it's worth confirming that constraint with your local municipality office before your architectural and structural drawings are finalized — not after.

Avoid These

Common Mistakes

  • Starting construction before the permit is issued. Even if a project is otherwise fully compliant, beginning work before formal approval creates legal exposure — stop-work orders and penalties are a real risk, not a theoretical one.
  • Submitting an incomplete document set. A missing stamp, an unsigned structural drawing, or an incomplete application form is enough to send the whole submission back — which is what actually extends the roughly 15-day timeline into something much longer.
